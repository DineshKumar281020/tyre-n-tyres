HOW DO YOU FIND OUT TYRE LIFE?

HOW DO YOU FIND OUT TYRE LIFE?

Tire life depends on factors like tread wear, driving conditions, and maintenance. Regularly check tread depth, air pressure, and alignment to extend lifespan. Driving habits, road quality, and climate also impact durability. Proper care ensures safety, performance, and cost efficiency.

Tyre Wear

Tyres often give their owners signs of problems in plenty of time to have them corrected. A person can easily read these early warning signs by looking for:

1. A saw tooth appearance on the edges

Erratic scrubbing against the road when a tyre is in need of a toe in or toe out alignment correction usually causes this.

2. Faster wear on the outer edges than in the middle

When a tyre is under inflated, more of its contact with the road is on the outer tread edges or the shoulder. Thus these areas wear faster than the middle. Be sure to check the tyres air pressure.

3. Quicker wear on the front or rear tyres on fwd vehicles

The rear tyres on a FWD vehicle may wear out faster due the light loads or misalignment. The front tyres on a FWD vehicle may wear out faster because they carry most of the weight and do most of the work. To obtain more even wear, the tyres may be rotated so all the tyres wear out at approximately the same mileage.

4. Tyres wears excessively on one side

Camber or toe in misalignment, which places too much of work on one side of the tyre, is usually to blame for one-sided wear. An alignment correction is required.

5. Cups or dips in the tread

Many things can cause this kind of irregular wear. Misalignment or worn out suspension parts are the most likely causes.

How do you find out the tyre life from the tread wear grade
written on the sidewall of the tyre

The tread wear grade can be used to estimate mileage or the tyre life a tyre can cover. For example take a grade number of 300 and multiply by 240kms per grade point and you would get 72,000 kms. (Tread wear grade x 240 kms per grade point = expected mileage). This is the number of kms that can be reasonably expected from a tyre when used in non-commercial service with a good driver and good maintenance.

For instance a 195 60 R 14 low profile tyre used in most of the mid segment cars, has a tread wear grade of 240. That denotes it can cover 57,600 kms.
